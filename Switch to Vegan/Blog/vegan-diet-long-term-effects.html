The question of whether a vegan diet is healthy long-term is one of the most-searched nutrition topics online — and also one of the most frequently misrepresented. Advocates claim it prevents nearly every chronic disease. Critics insist it's dangerously deficient. As usual, the truth that the actual research reveals is more nuanced than either extreme.

Over the past two decades, the volume of research into plant-based diets has grown substantially. Large-scale cohort studies tracking hundreds of thousands of people over many years — including the Adventist Health Study-2 (96,000+ participants) and the EPIC-Oxford study (47,000+ participants) — now give researchers substantial data to work with. Multiple systematic reviews and meta-analyses published in respected journals have since summarized these findings.

This article walks through what the peer-reviewed science actually shows — not cherry-picked headlines, but the complete picture, including the associations, the genuine risks, and the important limitations of current evidence.

First: A Note on Scientific Language

Before we go into the research, it's important to understand how science actually works — because misreading the language of studies is one of the most common ways nutrition information goes wrong.

📖 Imprecise vs. Accurate — How to Read Research Claims
❌ Imprecise / Overstated
"Vegan diets lead to significant reductions in cancer risk."
✅ Scientifically Accurate
"Vegan diets are associated with a reduced risk of certain cancers in observational studies."
❌ Imprecise / Overstated
"92% of vegans are deficient in B12."
✅ Scientifically Accurate
"High rates of B12 deficiency have been observed in vegans who do not supplement, with rates varying significantly by population and region."
❌ Imprecise / Overstated
"A vegan diet prevents heart disease."
✅ Scientifically Accurate
"Vegetarian and vegan diets are associated with lower cardiovascular disease risk in prospective cohort studies."

The key distinction is between association (two things appear together in a population) and causation (one thing directly causes another). Most long-term diet research is observational — it cannot prove direct causation. People who choose plant-based diets also tend to exercise more, smoke less, and drink less alcohol, which makes it difficult to isolate the diet's effect alone. We'll flag these limitations throughout.

The Major Long-Term Studies

Before examining specific health outcomes, here are the most important large-scale studies that form the evidence base for this topic:

Prospective Cohort · 96,000+ participants
Adventist Health Study-2 (AHS-2)
One of the largest long-running studies on plant-based diets, tracking participants across the US and Canada. Findings consistently show that vegetarian dietary patterns are associated with lower BMI, lower prevalence of type 2 diabetes, lower hypertension, and lower all-cause mortality. Vegan diets appear to confer additional benefit for obesity and hypertension compared to lacto-ovo-vegetarian diets. Researchers note that lifestyle factors common in this population — non-smoking, low alcohol — make it difficult to attribute outcomes to diet alone. [3]
→ Read original on PubMed/PMC (NIH)
Prospective Cohort · 47,000+ participants
EPIC-Oxford Study
A major European study tracking dietary patterns and health outcomes over many years. Vegans and vegetarians in EPIC-Oxford showed 11–19% lower risk for all cancers compared to non-vegetarians. Results on overall mortality were more mixed, with some analyses not demonstrating a significant all-cause mortality advantage for British vegetarians. The study also raised a specific concern: vegans showed a higher risk of hemorrhagic stroke, which researchers linked to lower B12 and possibly lower cholesterol levels. [1]
→ Read AHS-2 & EPIC-Oxford comparative review on PubMed
Systematic Review & Meta-Analysis · JAMA Network Open · 2023
Vegetarian Diets & Cardiometabolic Risk — JAMA Network Open
A systematic review and meta-analysis of 20 clinical trials published in JAMA Network Open (2023) examined vegetarian diets specifically in people with or at high risk of cardiovascular disease. It found that vegetarian dietary patterns were associated with improvements in key cardiometabolic markers — including LDL cholesterol, blood pressure, and blood glucose — compared to control diets. This is higher-quality evidence than observational cohorts alone, though still subject to limitations of small trial size. [5]
→ Read the meta-analysis on JAMA Network Open
Systematic Review & Meta-Analysis · Harvard T.H. Chan School · 2023
Plant-Based Diets, T2D, CVD, Cancer & Mortality — Harvard
A comprehensive systematic review and meta-analysis from the Department of Nutrition at Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health, updated in 2023, examined associations between plant-based dietary patterns and risks of type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, cancer, and mortality across a large body of evidence. Findings supported associations between healthier plant-based diets and lower risks across multiple chronic disease outcomes. [6]

Heart Disease & Cardiovascular Health

This is the area with the most consistent body of evidence in favour of plant-based diets. A 2022 systematic review and meta-analysis published in the European Journal of Nutrition — pooling data from 13 cohort studies covering 844,175 participants and over 115,000 CVD cases — found that vegetarian diets were associated with approximately 15% lower risk of cardiovascular disease and 21% lower risk of ischemic heart disease compared to non-vegetarian diets.[2]

The authors used World Cancer Research Fund (WCRF) criteria to assess causal strength and concluded that the associations between vegetarian diets, CVD, and ischemic heart disease were "probably causal" — a meaningful distinction in epidemiology, though still short of definitive proof.

❤️
Lower LDL Cholesterol
Vegan diets contain no dietary cholesterol (found only in animal products) and are high in soluble fiber, which actively helps lower LDL. Multiple studies consistently show vegans have lower total and LDL cholesterol than omnivores from similar backgrounds.
Consistent across multiple meta-analyses
🩸
Lower Blood Pressure
Observational studies show vegans tend to have lower blood pressure than non-vegans on average. High potassium from fruits and vegetables and lower sodium from avoiding processed meat products are plausible mechanisms — though lifestyle confounding remains a limitation.

Ischemic Heart Disease
The 2022 European Journal of Nutrition meta-analysis found vegetarian diets were associated with 21% lower IHD risk (RR 0.79, 95% CI: 0.71–0.88). The evidence for vegan diets specifically showed a summary RR of 0.82 for IHD, though with fewer studies and wider confidence intervals.

⚠️
Stroke Risk: Nuanced Finding
The EPIC-Oxford study observed a higher risk of hemorrhagic stroke among vegans — a finding that stands out amid otherwise favorable cardiovascular data. Researchers hypothesize that lower B12 levels and possibly lower cholesterol may be contributing factors. This highlights why supplementation genuinely matters.

What the evidence supports

Well-planned vegan and vegetarian diets are associated with lower risk of cardiovascular disease and ischemic heart disease across multiple large studies. This association is considered "probably causal" by WCRF standards. The hemorrhagic stroke finding is a genuine concern that reinforces the importance of consistent B12 supplementation.

Cancer — What the Data Actually Shows

Cancer research involving diet is inherently complex — cancer is not one disease, and dietary associations vary significantly by cancer type, population, and study design. With that context clearly stated, here is what current research shows:

A comprehensive 2024 umbrella review published in PLOS ONE — which analyzed systematic reviews and meta-analyses from 2000 to 2023 — found that vegetarian and vegan diets are associated with lower risk of ischemic heart disease and cancer. An earlier comprehensive meta-analysis found that a vegan diet was associated with approximately 15% lower incidence of total cancer compared to non-vegan diets.[4]

It is important to state what this does not mean: this does not indicate that a vegan diet causes cancer prevention, nor that it treats or cures cancer. These are population-level risk associations observed in observational studies. Individual biology, genetics, screening behavior, and lifestyle factors all play significant roles that these studies cannot fully account for.

The plausible mechanisms are well understood — plant-based diets are rich in fiber, antioxidants, and phytochemicals, while eliminating processed and red meats, which the World Health Organization classifies as carcinogens. But "associated with lower risk" and "prevents cancer" are meaningfully different claims, and the latter overstates what the evidence shows.

Type 2 Diabetes & Metabolic Health

This is one of the more robust areas of evidence, with supporting data from both observational studies and clinical trials. Across large cohort studies, vegetarian dietary patterns have been consistently associated with lower body mass index, lower prevalence and incidence of type 2 diabetes, and lower prevalence of metabolic syndrome components.[3]

The 2023 JAMA Network Open meta-analysis of 20 clinical trials found that vegetarian diets were associated with improvements in fasting blood glucose and glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c) in people with or at high risk of cardiovascular disease — adding higher-quality trial evidence alongside the observational data.[5]

A critical nuance

These associations and improvements are seen with whole-food plant-based diets — not "vegan junk food." A diet high in refined carbohydrates, sugar, and ultra-processed vegan products does not carry these benefits, and may carry its own risks. The quality of food choices matters as much as the category.

Body Weight & BMI

Across virtually every large study, people following vegan diets have lower average BMI than omnivores from similar backgrounds. This is likely due to a combination of lower dietary fat density, higher fiber intake increasing satiety, and lower overall caloric density of whole plant foods.

However, researchers consistently note that this association is confounded by health-consciousness: people who adopt vegan diets tend to be more health-aware in general. Controlled trials that standardize other lifestyle factors show smaller BMI differences. A poorly planned vegan diet high in refined oils, sugary drinks, and processed foods can absolutely lead to weight gain — the "vegan" label alone does not guarantee lower caloric intake.

Gut Health & the Microbiome

This is an emerging and genuinely promising area of research. Plant-based diets, being rich in diverse types of fiber and phytochemicals, appear to positively shape the gut microbiome in ways that may have broader health implications for immunity, inflammation, and mood.

Early research suggests that the diversity of fiber types from a wide variety of plant foods feeds a more diverse microbiome — and microbiome diversity is consistently associated (in observational research) with better immune function and reduced systemic inflammation. However, this remains an active area of study and direct causal pathways in humans are still being established. The research is promising, not conclusive.

The Real Risks: Nutrient Deficiencies

The research is equally clear on this side: an unplanned vegan diet carries genuine nutritional risks. These are not theoretical — they are well-documented in the medical literature and have been observed clinically in long-term vegans who do not supplement appropriately.[7, 8]

Crucially, every one of these risks is preventable with proper planning and supplementation. The following breakdown is based on current clinical evidence, not alarmism:

🧬
Vitamin B12 — The Non-Negotiable Risk
B12 is the most consistently documented and serious concern for vegans. It is found in reliable quantities only in animal products. Long-term deficiency causes megaloblastic anemia and irreversible neurological damage — including peripheral neuropathy and cognitive impairment. High rates of B12 deficiency have been observed in vegans who do not supplement, though reported rates vary significantly across studies and populations depending on diet composition and food fortification in the region. One European study observed deficiency in a majority of unsupplemented vegans — but researchers caution that rates depend heavily on local food fortification, dietary habits, and how "deficiency" is defined.[8]
✅ Solution: Supplement with cyanocobalamin (1000mcg daily, or 2000mcg twice weekly). This fully solves the problem. See the Vegan Society's evidence-based B12 guidance.
🦴
Bone Density & Fracture Risk
A 2025 systematic review published on PMC/NIH found consistent evidence linking poorly planned plant-based diets to reduced bone mineral density, particularly in women and older adults.[7] This is strongly linked to inadequate calcium, Vitamin D, and B12 — all manageable with proper planning. Importantly, vegans who meet calcium and Vitamin D requirements do not consistently show this increased fracture risk, making supplementation and food planning the determining factor rather than the diet category itself.
✅ Solution: Ensure adequate calcium through fortified plant milks, calcium-set tofu, kale, almonds, and sesame seeds. Supplement Vitamin D and take B12 consistently.
🩸
Iron Deficiency & Anaemia
Plant-based iron (non-heme iron) is less readily absorbed than heme iron from meat. Compounds such as phytates in grains and oxalates in certain vegetables can further inhibit absorption. The 2025 systematic review found consistent evidence linking poorly planned plant-based diets to increased risk of iron-deficiency anemia.[7] This risk is substantially mitigated by pairing iron-rich plant foods with Vitamin C, which significantly improves non-heme iron absorption.
✅ Solution: Eat iron-rich foods (lentils, tofu, pumpkin seeds, dark leafy greens) alongside a Vitamin C source (tomatoes, citrus, bell pepper). Avoid tea and coffee immediately before or after iron-rich meals, as tannins inhibit absorption.
🧠
Omega-3 Fatty Acids (DHA & EPA)
DHA and EPA are long-chain omega-3 fatty acids critical for brain function, cardiovascular health, and fetal neurological development. They are found primarily in fatty fish. While plant foods provide ALA (a shorter-chain omega-3 precursor found in flaxseeds, chia, and walnuts), the body's conversion of ALA to DHA and EPA is inefficient — typically below 10%. A 2025 review in Nutrients identified DHA and EPA deficiency as a key concern for long-term neurological health in people following vegan diets without supplementation.[9]
✅ Solution: Supplement with algae-based DHA/EPA — the original source from which fish derive their omega-3s. Also consume walnuts, flaxseeds, chia seeds, and hemp seeds regularly for ALA.
☀️
Vitamin D Deficiency
Vitamin D deficiency is widespread in the general population regardless of diet — but vegans face a marginally higher risk because oily fish (a common D source) and fortified dairy (another common source) are both excluded. Low Vitamin D is associated with reduced bone density, impaired immune function, low mood, and poor muscle function. Most people in countries with limited sunlight exposure are likely deficient during winter months.
✅ Solution: Get regular sunlight when possible. Supplement with Vitamin D3 (choose a lichen-derived vegan D3 supplement) during autumn and winter. A simple blood test can confirm your level.
⚡
Zinc & Iodine
Zinc from plant sources is less bioavailable due to phytate content in grains and legumes. Iodine — essential for thyroid function — is found mainly in seafood, iodized dairy, and seaweed. Vegans who do not use iodized salt and don't eat seaweed regularly may have insufficient iodine intake, potentially affecting thyroid health and metabolism. Both of these risks are manageable with straightforward dietary adjustments.
✅ Solution: Soak, sprout, or ferment legumes to reduce phytates and improve zinc bioavailability. Use iodized salt in cooking. Some dedicated vegan multivitamins include both zinc and iodine — check the label.

Bone Density — A Closer Look

Bone health deserves extended attention because the consequences of long-term calcium and Vitamin D insufficiency compound over decades and may only become apparent in later life through increased fracture risk.

A key finding from the research is that the risk is not inherent to veganism — it is specific to vegans who do not meet calcium, Vitamin D, and B12 requirements. A 2024 study in Frontiers in Nutrition found that when these nutrients were adequately consumed, bone mineral density in vegans was broadly comparable to omnivores.[10]

This reframes the question from "is veganism bad for bones?" to "do you have adequate calcium, D, and B12 intake?" — which is a question anyone, vegan or not, should be asking and monitoring through periodic blood tests.

Practical monitoring advice

If you follow a vegan diet long-term, consider asking your doctor for an annual blood panel that includes B12, Vitamin D (25-OH), full blood count (for anemia markers), and ferritin (iron stores). This is standard preventive care in many countries and gives you real data rather than guesswork about your nutritional status.

Brain Health & Neurological Function

A 2025 review in Nutrients — examining research published between 2010 and 2024 on vegan and vegetarian diets and neurological health — found a genuinely mixed picture.[9] On the positive side, diets rich in phytonutrients and antioxidants are associated with lower levels of inflammatory markers such as C-reactive protein, which may offer some protection against neuroinflammation and oxidative stress.

On the concerning side, the same review identified that inadequate B12 and DHA/EPA are risk factors for neurological impairment over time. B12 deficiency specifically is associated with subacute combined degeneration of the spinal cord — a serious, potentially irreversible neurological condition. Long-term studies directly comparing cognitive outcomes between supplemented vegans and omnivores are still limited, making strong conclusions premature in either direction.

"The potential neuroprotective effects of plant-rich diets and the neurotoxic effects of B12 deficiency can coexist — which is precisely why supplementation is inseparable from the dietary pattern itself."

Special Populations: Children & Pregnant Women

This is the area where the most caution is warranted, and where the evidence most strongly points toward the necessity of professional monitoring rather than self-guided nutrition decisions.

Pregnancy and breastfeeding

The nutrients of greatest concern during pregnancy on a vegan diet are B12, DHA, iodine, iron, folate, calcium, and Vitamin D. All of these can theoretically be met through a carefully planned vegan diet with appropriate supplementation — but the margin for error is narrower during pregnancy, and the consequences of deficiency extend to fetal development. The British Dietetic Association states that a well-planned vegan diet can be nutritionally adequate during pregnancy, while emphasizing that professional monitoring is essential.

Infants and children

The evidence in this group is more cautious. A scoping review published in Nutrients found that without proper supplementation and planning, vegan diets in children are associated with risks to physical and cognitive development, particularly through B12 deficiency.[8] The review noted that with adequate supplementation and monitoring, vegan diets can be made nutritionally adequate for children — but strongly recommended involvement of a registered dietitian and regular growth and blood monitoring. This is not a decision to manage without professional support.

Please seek professional guidance

If you are pregnant, breastfeeding, or considering a vegan diet for a child, please work with a registered dietitian before making dietary changes. The nutritional stakes are higher in these groups, the requirements are more complex, and the information in this article is explicitly not a substitute for individualized professional guidance.

The Honest Scientific Verdict

Based on the current weight of peer-reviewed evidence, a well-planned vegan diet is associated with meaningful health benefits for most adults, including lower risk of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, and certain cancers. These associations are consistent across multiple large cohort studies and have been supported by meta-analyses from reputable journals including JAMA and the European Journal of Nutrition.

At the same time, the evidence is equally clear that an unplanned vegan diet carries real nutritional risks — most critically B12 deficiency, which is universal without supplementation, and which can cause irreversible harm. Bone density, DHA/EPA levels, and iron status require ongoing attention.

The honest conclusion is this: a well-planned, supplemented vegan diet appears to be healthy long-term for most adults based on available evidence. "Well-planned" is not optional language — it is the operative phrase that determines whether the benefits materialise or the risks accumulate. Supplement B12 without exception. Eat a wide variety of whole plant foods. Get blood tests periodically. Consult a dietitian for complex situations. With those practices in place, the evidence suggests most people can thrive on a plant-based diet long-term.

As always: this conclusion is based on current evidence as of 2025. Nutrition science evolves. Individual health needs vary. Please consult a qualified healthcare professional for personalised advice.
