How it started

A video I
couldn't un-see

It wasn't a conversation, a book, or a health scare. It was a video — the kind that arrives without warning — showing how animals are treated inside the dairy industry. Not in some faraway factory. By local suppliers. The kind most people, including me, had always assumed were fine.

I couldn't absorb what I saw and keep doing what I was doing. So I decided I wanted to stop contributing to it. That part was simple. Everything that came after was not.

"I didn't go vegan. I started trying to go vegan. Those felt like two very different things — and that distinction made all the difference for me."

The first few weeks were genuinely disorienting. I didn't know what to eat in the morning. I didn't know how to explain it at the dinner table. Every source I found online contradicted the last. I knew what I didn't want to do — I just didn't know how to actually do anything differently.

That confusion — and finding my own slow, imperfect way through it — is the entire reason this website exists.

The start is rarely dramatic. It's usually just a quiet moment of noticing something you can't stop noticing.

The real process

Slow. Imperfect.
And it worked.

The thing that actually helped

"Not today. Maybe tomorrow."

The most important thing I noticed in myself: the harder I told myself "I can never eat this again," the more my mind fixated on exactly that thing. For me, fighting it head-on made it worse. So I tried delaying instead. When I craved ice cream, I told myself: not today — maybe tomorrow. Tomorrow came, and I said it again. Over weeks, the craving got quieter. It stopped feeling like deprivation and started feeling like a choice I was making, at my own pace, for my own reasons.

1

Stopped ordering dairy outside first

Restaurants gave me control without disrupting anything at home. A small, private decision — and for me, that felt far easier to keep than any dramatic announcement.

2

Reduced cheese and desserts gradually — not all at once

One swap at a time, at my pace. I didn't declare anything. I just started choosing differently, occasionally, then more often. Progress without performance.

3

Stopped dairy at home over many months

The hardest part wasn't food — it was routine. Morning chai. Familiar comfort. I had to find alternatives I actually liked, not just tolerated, for any of it to last.

4

Started reading actual research instead of wellness blogs

For me, switching from random wellness blogs to peer-reviewed sources — registered dietitians, academic institutions, published studies — made nutrition feel less scary and more navigable. I stopped feeling overwhelmed and started feeling curious.

5

Learned veganism extends beyond food

Leather, silk, honey, cosmetics tested on animals — most beginners don't know this at the start. I didn't either. That's not a failure. It's just how learning works: in layers, over time, without a deadline.

Why this exists

I needed a resource
that didn't exist yet.

Transitioning to veganism in India has specific friction points that most vegan resources I found never addressed — because they weren't built with this context in mind. Personally, the nutrition questions felt overwhelming. Vegan alternatives were expensive and hard to find. Indian cooking is built around dairy in ways that aren't easy to work around. Social situations carry weight that goes beyond food. And figuring out which sources online to actually trust took more energy than I had when I was already confused.

"I didn't start this to become a vegan authority. I started it because the calm, practical, India-aware resource I was looking for didn't seem to exist — so I started putting one together."

I struggled personally with ice cream cravings that felt impossible to reason with. With not knowing how to get enough protein for the gym. With expensive almond milk and no affordable alternative. With conflicting things I'd read about B12, omega-3, and iron — none of which agreed with each other. With Indian family dinners. With not finding a single recipe site that cooked with the ingredients actually available in an Indian kitchen, at an Indian budget.

Every tool, guide, and article on this site came from one of those specific frustrations I ran into. A meal planner that tries to work with Indian ingredients. Nutrition information that links back to published research rather than opinion. Affordable local alternatives where I could find them. Honest attempts at the questions I kept hitting. Not inspiration. Just trying to be practically useful.

This site is not about being perfect. It is not about shame. It is not about converting anyone. It's about making an already difficult transition a little less confusing — for people who are somewhere in the middle of figuring it out, exactly as I was.

What this place stands for

Five things
that won't
change here.

Not marketing copy. The actual principles this site was built around — and the things I wish I'd found earlier.

01

No shame. Not ever.

You will never be made to feel guilty for where you are. Slow transitions count. Slipping up counts. Starting again counts. All of it counts.

02

Research over opinion.

Where possible, nutrition information on this site links back to published research rather than personal opinion. When something is uncertain or debated, the aim is to say so clearly rather than overstate what's known.

03

India first, always.

Grocery lists, recipes, protein sources, social situations — built around Indian kitchens, Indian budgets, Indian families, and Indian culture. Not retrofitted from elsewhere.

04

Practical over inspirational.

Inspiration is easy to find. A clear, specific answer on a Tuesday evening when you don't know what to cook is much harder. That's what this tries to be.

05

Your pace. Not anyone else's.

There is no right speed. No correct version. The only thing that matters is that you keep going — in whatever way fits your life, your family, your reality.

How content is approached

What this site tries
to be honest about.

I am not a doctor, dietitian, or researcher. I'm someone who went through this transition, got confused, and tried to find better information. Here's how I try to approach the content on this site — and where its limits are.

1

Links to published sources where possible

When nutrition information is shared, I try to link it back to published research — institutions like the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics, Harvard T.H. Chan, or the NHS — so you can read the original source yourself rather than just taking my word for it.

2

Tries to be honest when things are uncertain

A lot of nutrition science is genuinely contested or still evolving. The aim here is to say "this isn't fully settled" when that's true, rather than sounding more confident than the research actually supports.

3

Updated when things change or improve

Guidance changes over time. I try to revisit content when I become aware it's outdated, and articles carry a "last reviewed" date where possible. It won't always be perfect — but the intention is for it to stay accurate.

4

Not a substitute for professional advice

This is a personal website, not a medical or clinical resource. If you have a health condition, are pregnant, or have specific nutritional concerns, please speak with a qualified doctor or registered dietitian — not a website.

i

To be completely clear: I'm not a doctor, dietitian, or nutrition professional. I'm someone who went through this transition, got confused by a lot of conflicting information, tried to find better sources, and wanted to share what helped me. Everything here is personal experience combined with my best attempt to find reliable information — not professional advice. Please treat it as a starting point, and always consult a qualified professional for anything health-related.
