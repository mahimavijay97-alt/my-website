Evidence & Findings

The research behind vegan & plant-based diets

You don't need to read scientific studies to start making changes — but if you're curious, here's what current research suggests, in clear language.

This page brings together findings from peer-reviewed studies and major health institutions, simplified into clear insights. It is not a medical resource; it is a calm overview of where the evidence is strong, where it is mixed, and what that means in everyday terms.

Health

What Research Suggests About Health

Seven major areas where plant-based diets have been studied — what the evidence shows, what it means in practice, and where to be cautious about the findings.

All-Cause Mortality

Strong evidence

A large 2025 meta-analysis of nearly one million participants found that adherence to a healthy plant-based diet was associated with a 15% lower risk of all-cause mortality. Critically, the same analysis showed no benefit — and in some patterns, higher risk — for unhealthy plant-based diets built around refined grains, sugary drinks, and ultra-processed foods.

Source Meta-analysis of 977,763 participants (2025) — adherence to healthy vs. unhealthy plant-based diets and all-cause mortality risk.

What this means: Diet quality matters more than the label “plant-based” alone. A whole-food vegan diet shows meaningful longevity benefits; a junk-food vegan diet does not.

Caution: This is observational evidence (associations, not direct causation), and individual outcomes depend on many other lifestyle factors.

Cardiovascular Health

Strong evidence

Research indicates that plant-based diets are associated with improvements in several key heart health markers, including cholesterol levels, blood pressure, and inflammation.

Source American Journal of Preventive Cardiology (2024) — umbrella review on plant-based diets & cardiovascular outcomes (associations with reduced CVD risk and improvements in LDL cholesterol and BMI).

What this means: Plant-based diets may support heart health over time.

Caution: Most findings are based on observational studies, meaning they show associations rather than direct cause-and-effect.

Type 2 Diabetes

Strong evidence

A large meta-analysis found that higher adherence to plant-based diets was associated with a lower risk of type 2 diabetes.

Source JAMA Internal Medicine (2019) — meta-analysis of plant-based dietary patterns and incidence of type 2 diabetes.

What this means: Plant-based diets — especially those focused on whole foods — may support better blood sugar regulation.

Caution: Diet quality matters. Highly processed plant-based diets do not show the same benefits.

Body Weight

Moderate evidence

Research shows that plant-based diets are associated with modest but consistent weight reduction in clinical settings.

Source Journal of the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics (2015) — review of plant-based diets and weight outcomes in clinical trials.

What this means: These diets may support weight management, often due to higher fibre and lower calorie density.

Caution: Long-term maintenance depends on overall lifestyle and consistency.

Cancer Risk

Mixed evidence

Research on cancer risk shows inconsistent and site-specific findings. Some studies suggest reduced risk for certain cancers, while others show no difference or mixed results.

Source British Journal of Cancer (2026) — large pooled analysis reporting varied outcomes depending on cancer type.

What this means: There is currently no clear scientific consensus linking vegan diets to overall cancer risk reduction.

Caution: Cancer risk depends on many factors beyond diet, including genetics, environment, and lifestyle.

Bone Health

Needs attention

Studies including the EPIC-Oxford cohort have found that vegans may have lower bone mineral density and a higher fracture risk than people who include animal products.

Source Nutrition Reviews (2019) — review of vegan diets and bone health outcomes. Source EPIC-Oxford cohort study, BMC Medicine (2020) — fracture risk in meat eaters, fish eaters, vegetarians, and vegans.

What this means: Bone health requires attention in vegan diets — particularly through adequate calcium, vitamin D, and protein.

Caution: This risk may be influenced by calcium, vitamin D, protein intake, and overall diet quality, not veganism on its own.

Environmental Impact

Strong evidence

Research strongly suggests that plant-based diets have a lower environmental footprint, with significantly lower greenhouse gas emissions, land use, and water use compared to high-meat diets.

Source Nature Food (2023) — large-scale modelling study comparing the environmental impact of vegan, vegetarian, and meat-based diets.

What this means: Dietary choices can have a measurable impact on environmental sustainability.

Caution: Environmental impact varies depending on specific foods and production methods.

Nutrition

Nutritional Considerations

A vegan diet can be nutritionally adequate, but a handful of nutrients require attention. The science on each is summarized below.

Vitamin B12

Research shows that B12 deficiency is a consistent risk in unsupplemented vegan diets, since B12 is not reliably found in plant foods.

What this means: B12 supplementation is essential for vegans.

View source on PubMed

Iron

Plant-based (non-heme) iron has lower absorption compared to animal-based iron, though deficiency is not universal among vegans.

What this means: Iron intake should be considered, especially alongside vitamin C-rich foods for better absorption.

View source on ScienceDirect

Omega-3

The conversion of plant-based omega-3 (ALA) into the longer-chain forms EPA and DHA is limited in the body.

What this means: Some individuals may benefit from algae-based omega-3 supplements.

View source on PMC

Iodine

Research consistently shows that iodine intake is significantly lower in vegan populations — with systematic reviews finding the lowest urinary iodine of all dietary groups, often well below WHO optimal range. This is particularly concerning for women of childbearing age, where adequate iodine is critical for fetal brain development.

What this means: Iodine intake should be actively monitored. Reliable sources include iodised salt, small amounts of seaweed (in moderation, as some are very high), and supplements where appropriate.

View source on PMC

Protein

Research suggests that protein intake is generally adequate with a varied plant-based diet — the “protein problem” is not as common as widely believed.

What this means: Protein needs can be met with proper food choices — beans, lentils, tofu, tempeh, whole grains, nuts, and seeds.

View source on PubMed

Vitamin D & Calcium

While not as tightly studied in isolated vegan trials, both nutrients matter — particularly given the bone-health findings noted above.

What this means: Sunlight, fortified plant milks, leafy greens, and supplements (where appropriate) are commonly recommended.

View NHS guidance

Beyond Health

Ethical & System-Level Context

Beyond nutrition and personal health, research also looks at the wider systems that food choices interact with.

Animal Agriculture & Welfare

Documented data

Global data from organisations such as the FAO indicate that billions of animals are raised annually for food production. Research also highlights that industrial farming systems prioritise efficiency, which can influence animal welfare and environmental impact.

Source MDPI Agriculture (2024) — analysis of welfare and efficiency trade-offs in industrial animal farming systems.

What this means: Ethical considerations are one of the reasons people choose veganism, but interpretations of the data vary by individual.

Honest Summary

Where Research Is Strong vs. Uncertain

Not all findings carry the same weight. This is a quick, honest summary of where the evidence is consistent — and where it is still mixed.

Strong agreement

  • Lower all-cause mortality (healthy plant-based diets)
  • Cardiovascular health improvements
  • Lower risk of type 2 diabetes
  • Lower environmental impact
  • Need for B12 supplementation

Mixed or uncertain

  • Cancer risk
  • Long-term outcomes across all populations
  • Specific nutrient impacts in different groups
  • Bone health (depends on overall diet quality)

Self-Research

Recommended Search Strategy for Self-Research

If you'd like to search the evidence yourself on PubMed, these terms and filters will help you find the most reliable, peer-reviewed studies.

Best search terms

Copy and paste any of these directly into the PubMed search bar.

  • "plant-based diet" AND "systematic review"[pt]
  • "vegan diet" AND "meta-analysis"[pt]
  • "vegetarian diet" AND "randomized controlled trial"[pt]
  • "plant-based diet" AND "cardiovascular disease" AND "2020:2026"[dp]

Best filters to apply

Use the filters panel on the left side of PubMed results.

  • Article type Systematic Review or Meta-Analysis — these summarise many studies at once.
  • Publication date Last 5 years — to focus on the most current evidence.
  • Free full text Tick this to access open-access papers you can read in full.

Tip: Reading the abstract and conclusion sections first is usually enough to understand the main findings — you don't need to read every page.

Final perspective

You don't need to understand every study to make informed choices.

Research can guide you — but it doesn't need to overwhelm you.

A balanced approach and basic awareness are enough to start. The studies above will keep evolving, and your understanding can evolve with them — at your own pace, without pressure.
